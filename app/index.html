Market context

Security investment should show up before the incident, not only during the post-mortem.

Many organizations still depend on reactive controls: mempool surveillance, manual incident response, or protocol-wide pause switches. These remain necessary, but they typically engage after a transaction is already in motion—when treasury and reputation risk are hardest to unwind.

At the same time, delivery pressure is rising: faster release cycles, broader integration surfaces, and audit capacity that cannot cover every change at the speed of shipping. The gap between “we were audited once” and “we are safe today” keeps widening.

Moat focuses control at the transaction boundary—on the functions that move value—before final state is committed on-chain.

Benefits

A control layer stakeholders can understand: explicit policy, visible enforcement, and an accountable path when decisions are disputed.

Stakeholder assurance

Communicate how critical entry points are protected: which functions are gated, what policy applies, and how end users retain recourse if a decision is wrong—supporting diligence from partners and liquidity providers.

Protocol-specific policy

You author the invariants that matter to your economics—limits, ratios, yield bands, frequency caps. Evaluation is against your model, not a generic vendor risk score.

Transparent architecture

Moat composes established building blocks—on-chain enforcement patterns such as the Forta Firewall proxy, plus decentralized attestation from an operator set—so integration choices remain reviewable and auditable.

Solution overview

Standard submission paths or a dedicated watchtower feed the same policy engine: attest, then execute—or wait through a defined delay.

Traffic may arrive via public RPC or, where latency is sensitive, through a Moat watchtower. Independent operators simulate the proposed state transition, execute your rule set, and co-sign attestations. A Forta Firewall–style on-chain modifier applies the result before protected contract logic runs.

  • Pre-execution review: surface policy violations before state is finalized on-chain.
  • Distributed attestation: aligned operators, shared rules—no single point of policy failure.
  • Governed exception path: configurable delay gives operators time to respond while preserving user exit.

Risk landscape: OWASP Smart Contract Top 10

The OWASP Foundation maintains the Smart Contract Top 10 (2026 edition), widely used as a common taxonomy for on-chain contract risk. Its maintainers describe how the ranking is built from 2025 incident telemetry and practitioner input—their methodology note explains the aggregation model, and the data sources page lists what went into the analysis. The chart below highlights four categories that recur in public incident write-ups; bar length reflects relative incident counts in this illustrative slice (not dollar loss).

Each bar is scaled to the count shown on the right (the largest value in this view, 47, fills the track). For authoritative ordering, full category definitions, and the complete 2026 list, refer to the published ranking on OWASP’s Smart Contract Security site.

Business logic vulnerabilities (SC02)

47 attacks mapped to absent sanity checks and flawed economic logic in public analyses. Example: Resolv—roughly $25M when broken minting logic reportedly allowed tens of millions of unbacked tokens against minimal collateral (~$200K), a classic “looks fine at the opcode level, wrong at the system level” failure.

Price oracle manipulation (SC03)

29 attacks tied to manipulable or misused price feeds. Venus Protocol lost on the order of $2M when THE token pricing was exploited—oracle assumptions, not just swap math, were the lever.

Reentrancy (SC08)

13 incidents in this bucket despite reentrancy being textbook since The DAO. Solv lost about $2.7M from a double-minting / reentrancy-style path—state not finalized before external calls completed.

Flash loan–facilitated attacks (SC04)

Makina Finance—about $5M drained using a $280M flash loan to warp oracle pricing in a single transaction: a small pricing bug amplified into a treasury event.

Audit coverage gap

In their own documentation for the 2026 list, OWASP cites roughly 122 deduplicated smart-contract incidents and about $905M in associated losses for the analyzed slice—figures published alongside the project’s data sources. Even that scoped sample spans many teams, which underscores how often production diverges from the snapshot a single audit represents.

Third-party surveys and hack compendiums (for example Halborn’s review of major DeFi incidents) similarly report that a limited share of exploited code paths were covered by a prior, in-scope audit—exact percentages differ by study, but the structural issue is consistent: release cadence and integration change faster than periodic review cycles.

Moat is intended to narrow that gap through continuous, transaction-level evaluation against policies you own—complementing formal audits when design or deployment has moved on since the last sign-off.
